Most people who write about systems have studied them.

They read Seneca in college dorms. They annotated Marcus Aurelius in grad school libraries. They found Machiavelli on a syllabus and wrote papers about what he meant. I respect that, I've done the reading too.

But there's a category of understanding that books can't give you, and that's the understanding you get from being inside a system that was never designed with your survival in mind. That's the kind I have, and if you're going to read anything I write, you should know where it comes from.

I. Queensbridge Didn't See Me Coming

I grew up in Queensbridge. If you know New York, you know what that means. If you don't, Queensbridge is the largest public housing projects in North America. Long Island City, Queens. Right across the water from Midtown Manhattan, close enough to see the skyline but far enough that the skyline wasn't looking back at you.

The culture was Black. The music was Black. The language, the code, the survival logic, all of it rooted in a Black experience that I was surrounded by but, in the most literal biological sense, was not fully of.

I'm not Black. I'm Honduran, and having green eyes with fair skin definitely didn't make it any easier.

I grew up in a building where almost everyone was black. I played ball on courts where almost everyone was. I moved through a world where the social contract was written in a language built specifically for people navigating anti-Blackness in America, and I was a non-Black kid code-switching before I even had a word for it.

That position, never fully inside, never fully outside, turned me into an observer before I was old enough to know that was a skill.

I didn't experience Queensbridge the way a Black kid from Queensbridge experienced it. I want to be honest about that. The weight of that identity, the generational pressure, the specific way the system targeted people who looked like my friends, that wasn't mine to carry the same way.

But I was there. I was in the rooms. I watched how power moved. I watched who got stopped on the corner and who didn't. I watched who the school counselors wrote off and who got "potential" stamped on their file. I watched the informal economy operate with more organizational clarity than anything I'd see in corporate America a decade later.

And because I was adjacent, not at the center, I could see the architecture.

When you're at the center of a system, you feel it. When you're at the edge of one, you see it.

I've been at the edge of every room I've ever entered. That's not a disadvantage. That's a lens.

II. The GPA Nobody Expected

Here's the thing about growing up in the hood and graduating with a 4.2 GPA: nobody believes both can be true at the same time.

People hear "Queensbridge" and they build a picture. People hear "4.2" and they build a different one. When you tell them those two pictures are the same person, there's a beat of cognitive dissonance, a system error in the way they've categorized the world.

That error is instructive.

It means they've been running on flawed data. It means the mental model they've built about who comes from where and what they're capable of is broken. And most people, rather than updating their model, will find a way to resolve the dissonance without changing what they believe.

They'll call you exceptional. What they mean is: you're the exception that proves the rule. They want to honor you without auditing the framework.

I refuse that deal.

The GPA wasn't despite where I came from. It was because of it.

In the hood, you learn to read rooms fast or you pay for it. You learn which adults are actually trying to help you and which ones are managing liability. You learn that institutional authority and legitimate authority are two very very different things, and that confusing them is dangerous.

Those are systems thinking skills. I just didn't have the vocabulary for it yet.

School gave me the vocabulary. The projects gave me the data. I graduated knowing how to do both: read the textbook and read the room. Most people in academic environments only know one of those.

That's not a small thing. The ability to move between frameworks, to understand a system from the inside and analyze it from the outside simultaneously, that's rare, and it's not something you develop in a comfortable environment.

Comfort doesn't develop range. Friction does.

III. What the Streets Actually Teach You

I want to be precise here, because romanticizing the streets is its own kind of dishonesty.

The streets are not a school. They're a pressure system. And what pressure does is reveal structure, both in external environments and in yourself.

What I learned moving through that world wasn't hustle mythology. It wasn't "grind culture" or the bootstraps narrative that gets recycled every time someone from a hard background makes it somewhere. That's not the lesson.

The lesson was hierarchy.

Not the sanitized org-chart version of hierarchy that business schools teach. Real hierarchy, how it forms, how it holds, how it fails. Who actually has power versus who's just occupying a powerful position. What happens when the wrong person holds authority too long. What fills the vacuum when a system's legitimacy collapses.

The streets run on the same logic as every institution I've encountered since. The titles are different. The stakes are different. The underlying architecture is identical.

I've sat in boardrooms and recognized the same social dynamics I watched play out on corners. The same posturing. The same informal hierarchies operating under the surface of official ones. The same gap between what's said in public and what's decided in back rooms.

The difference is that in the streets, the consequences for misreading a situation are immediate and physical. That sharpens your perception in ways that polite professional environments don't.

I'm not saying danger is a teacher I'd recommend. I'm saying it was the teacher I had. And what I learned from it, how to read power structures, how to track alignment between stated values and actual behavior, how to identify which rules are enforced and which are just decorative, that's been directly applicable to every system I've analyzed since.

Including philosophy. Including history. Including the structures I write about every week.

IV. The Cell Was a Whiteboard

I've debated how much to say here, and I've landed on this: I'm not going to narrate the specific contours of what landed me where I've been. The details aren't the point. The architecture is.

What I will say is this: I've been inside, and being inside is the most complete system immersion I've ever experienced.

Prison is a total institution. It regulates time, space, movement, language, social organization, access to resources, everything. And the thing about total institutions is that they expose every assumption you've been operating with about how the world works.

Outside, you can maintain the illusion that your choices exist in a vacuum. That you're exercising genuine autonomy. That the structures around you are neutral, or at least benign.

Inside, that illusion evaporates.

You see, with perfect clarity, that systems produce behavior, not the other way around. That the environment shapes the person more than the person shapes the environment. That the rules governing a space determine what's possible inside it, regardless of individual intention.

That's Marcus Aurelius's insight. That's Baldwin's insight. That's what Machiavelli was actually writing about when he wasn't being misread as a cynicism manual. They were all pointing at the same thing: the relationship between the individual and the structure they inhabit.

I understood those thinkers differently after that experience. Not because I was suffering, and not because suffering is somehow aggrandizing, that's another romanticization I want toreject. But because I had sat inside a system with no ability to pretend it wasn't a system, and once you've had that experience, you can't unsee it.

Every institution becomes legible in a new way. The hospital. The corporation. The government. The family. The church. They're all systems with rules, hierarchies, incentive structures, and gaps between their stated purpose and their actual function.

I see those gaps clearly. I see them because I had to.

Necessity is a more rigorous teacher than curiosity.

V. What Outsider Status Actually Costs, And What It Pays

I want to complicate the narrative here before it gets too clean.

The "outsider as observer" framework I'm describing has real costs. It's not a gift you receive without paying for it.

Being adjacent to multiple worlds and fully at home in none of them produces a specific kind of isolation. You spend years translating yourself, to different communities, different environments, different expectations. You become fluent in multiple languages while wondering if you actually belong in any of them.

That's not romantic. That's genuinely disorienting, especially when you're young .

I'm describing it now with the perspective of someone who has worked out what that position means. I didn't have that perspective at the time. At the time, it was just the texture of daily life, the slight vertigo of being a person who doesn't fit cleanly into any given category.

But here's what that dislocation pays: it pays in pattern recognition. When you've never been able to just absorb a room without question, you develop the habit of reading it.

People who grow up fully embedded in a dominant culture absorb its assumptions without noticing. The water is invisible to the fish. The ideology is invisible to its beneficiary.

I've rarely had that luxury. And so I've rarely stopped noticing the water.

That's not a virtue I earned, it's a condition I was placed in. But I've chosen to treat it as a tool rather than a grievance. And that choice, made repeatedly over years, is what produced whatever clarity I actually have.

VI. Why Any of This Makes Me Worth Listening To

I don't believe credentials alone create authority. A degree doesn't make you right. A title doesn't make your framework accurate. Neither does suffering, for the record. Suffering doesn't produce wisdom automatically, it produces experience. What you do with that experience determines whether wisdom follows.

So this is not me saying: I've been through things, therefore listen to me.

This is me saying something more specific.

The figures I write about in The Cast Newsletter: Marcus Aurelius, James Baldwin, Niccolo Machiavelli, and the others, they all earned their insight through position. Through the specific vantage point that their life circumstances forced them to occupy.

Marcus Aurelius was emperor. The weight of holding a system together while trying not to be corrupted by it, that wasn't a thought experiment for him. It was his daily reality.

Baldwin was a Black man in mid-century America who understood both the inside and outside of Western culture simultaneously, because he was claimed by it intellectually and excluded from it politically.

Machiavelli watched the Florentine republic collapse. He wasn't theorizing about power. He was doing a post-mortem.

What I'm doing in this newsletter is applying the same method: using a specific, earned vantage point to analyze how systems actually work versus how they claim to work.

My vantage point is this: I grew up at the intersection of poverty and academic achievement, inside a culture I was adjacent to but not identical with, and I have sat inside the most total institution the state deploys and come out the other side thinking harder, not less.

That combination produces a specific kind of legibility, the ability to see sytsems from multiple angles simultaneously because I've occupied multiple positions within them.

That's what I bring to these pages.

Not a credential. Not a title. Not the borrowed authority of citing the right names. A lens, built through pressure and position, that I am now consciously deploying on the ideas that have structured human civilization.

You can decide whether that's worth your time. But you should know what it is.

VII. The Work, Not the Wound

I'll close here with something important.

I am not interested in my story as a story. I'm interested in my story as infrastructure, as the foundation that makes the analysis I do possible.

There's a version of this essay that wallows. That performs pain. That asks you to feel something for the kid in the projects or the man in the cell, and then leverages your sympathy into your attention.

That's not what this is.

What I just described to you isn't context for pity. It's context for evaluation. I'm asking you to assess the quality of the lens, not to feel sorry for how it was ground.

The Cast Newsletter is a systems audit. Every piece I write is an attempt to look clearly at how power, hierarchy, behavior, and consequence actually operate, stripped of sentimentality, ideology, and the comfortable fictions institutions use to legitimate themselves.

I can do that work because I've been forced to see clearly in environments where seeing clearly was a survival requirement, not an intellectual preference.

That's the credential.

It's not a degree, or the GPA, though I earned that too. It wasn't the suffering, which I don't romanticize. The capacity to see, built through years of being in rooms where softening the truth was a luxury I couldn't afford.

I can afford a lot more now. I still choose not to.

That's the commitment I'm making to you every time I publish.

Come audit the infrastructure with me.
